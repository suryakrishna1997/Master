Modern vehicles aren't one computer — they're a distributed system of 70–150 small computers (ECUs) talking over a handful of shared buses. This reference walks through the sensing layer, the infotainment layer, the control layer, and the wiring that ties them together, the same way you'd trace a signal on the bench.

01

Camera & Vision Systems

The eyes of the vehicle. Each camera feeds an ADAS ECU, a domain controller, or a zonal controller depending on the platform, typically over GMSL, FPD-Link, or Automotive Ethernet. None of these are "just a camera" — each is a full sensing pipeline: optics, image sensor, image signal processing, time synchronization, power management, self-diagnostics, and calibration all have to work together before a single frame reaches the driver's screen. A camera that is perfectly sharp but even a few milliseconds out of sync with the rest of the system can still fail validation.

RVC — Rear View Camera

reverse

The standard backup camera used while reversing. It shows a fairly normal (non-wide) field of view directly behind the bumper, so the driver can judge close obstacles accurately.

It's usually triggered automatically the instant reverse gear is engaged, and it appears on the head unit with parking guidelines and dynamic trajectory lines overlaid on top.

Think of it as the baseline safety camera — every other rear camera variant is really a wider or smarter version of this one.

RWC — Rear Wide Camera

reverse

A wider-angle rear camera. Compared to a standard RVC it captures more of the area behind the car, which helps a lot when reversing out of tight spots or scanning for cross-traffic.

Some OEMs treat RWC and RVC as two separate, distinctly named functions; others use the terms almost interchangeably for the same physical camera with a wider lens fitted.

The tradeoff is coverage versus distortion — a wider lens sees more, but the edges of the image bend more too, so overlay graphics have to be calibrated carefully.

MVC / SVC — Surround View

360°

The bird's-eye 360° system built from four fisheye cameras — front, rear, and both door mirrors — stitched into one top-down composite view for parking and low-speed maneuvers.

The real engineering challenge isn't the stitching algorithm alone; it's calibration accuracy, frame-timing alignment across four separate sensors, and blending the seams so the result looks like one continuous image rather than four overlapping ones.

This is a textbook case of a system that lives or dies on synchronized sensor-to-ECU communication — even small timing drift between cameras shows up as a visible "ghost line" in the stitched view.

LaneWatch Camera

Honda

Honda's passenger-side blind-spot camera, mounted near the side mirror. It is camera-based, not radar-based — it shows a live video feed of the passenger lane instead of just beeping.

It typically activates when the turn signal is used or a dedicated mirror button is pressed, giving the driver visual confirmation of traffic or cyclists before changing lanes.

Its main limitation is one-sidedness: it only covers the passenger side, so it complements — rather than replaces — a full blind-spot monitoring system.

FVC — Front View Camera

forward

The forward-facing camera behind the windshield, used for lane detection, traffic sign recognition, object detection, and sometimes traffic-light recognition — arguably the single most important vision input for driver-assist perception.

It usually works in tandem with front radar: the camera classifies what an object is, and the radar measures how far away it is and how fast it's closing, which is a more reliable combination than either sensor alone.

Because it sits near the rear-view mirror, a cracked or dirty windshield in that exact spot can knock out lane-keeping and forward-collision warning even if nothing else on the car is wrong.

DMS — Driver Monitoring

in-cabin

Watches the driver, not the road. An in-cabin camera — often infrared-assisted so it still works at night — tracks gaze direction, eyelid closure, head pose, and distraction or drowsiness cues.

This matters because even fairly advanced driver-assist systems still legally and practically require an attentive supervising driver, so DMS is what keeps that assumption honest.

On a fault, it typically escalates gradually — a soft chime first, then a visual warning, then in some systems a request for the driver to physically confirm they're still in control.

OMS — Occupant Monitoring

in-cabin

Monitors who and what is inside the cabin — seat occupancy, child presence, posture, and seatbelt-related checks — rather than watching only the driver.

Depending on the OEM it may directly influence airbag deployment logic, working closely with the SRS module so a child seat or an empty seat doesn't get treated the same as an adult occupant.

Like DMS it can use visible light, infrared, or a blend of both, and privacy/lighting constraints usually decide which approach a given OEM picks.

02

Radar, Ultrasonic & LiDAR

Where cameras classify, these sensors measure — distance, closing speed, and object presence, and they do it reliably in rain, fog, glare, and darkness where a camera alone struggles. In a real ADAS stack, radar range/velocity data and camera classification are fused together so the system isn't fully dependent on either sensor type; that redundancy is exactly why false positives drop sharply once fusion is added.

MRR / SRR — Mid & Short Range Radar

corners

Used for blind-spot monitoring, lane-change assist, and rear cross-traffic alert. SRR units usually sit in the rear (and sometimes front) corners, exactly where adjacent-lane and crossing traffic is easiest to catch.

MRR extends that range a little further and contributes to more advanced side-looking perception, while SRR stays focused on the tighter, closer-in cases.

Radar's real superpower here is relative speed — it measures closing velocity accurately even in conditions where a camera's classification confidence would normally drop.

LRR — Long-Range Radar

forward

Powers adaptive cruise control and forward collision support by looking much farther down the road than SRR/MRR, focused specifically on vehicles in the ego lane at highway speed.

Its core outputs are range, velocity, and sometimes angle — enough for the control system to hold a safe following distance and react before the driver would notice a change.

Paired with the FVC, LRR goes from "there's something ahead" to "that's a car, four lanes over, closing at 12 km/h" — the fusion is what makes the system trustworthy.

USS — Ultrasonic Sensors

parking

The short-range "parking ears" mounted around the bumpers. Cheap, simple, and very good at detecting nearby objects at low speed — exactly the job parking assist needs.

They're strong for stall detection, wall distance, and curb detection, but they're not built for long range or high-speed perception, so you won't find them doing highway-speed work.

Their one real weakness is soft or angled surfaces — a foam bollard or a shallow curb can return a weaker echo than a flat wall, which is a classic USS validation edge case.

LiDAR

3D map

Uses laser pulses to build a high-resolution 3D map of the environment, giving much finer spatial detail than radar can offer, at the cost of higher price and tighter packaging constraints.

In high-end ADAS and autonomous stacks it's valued specifically for object shape and depth understanding — telling a pedestrian's silhouette apart from a signpost, for example.

It's rarely used as the only perception sensor; it's almost always one voice in a larger sensor-fusion stack alongside camera and radar.

03

Infotainment & Connectivity

This layer is about experience and connectivity — media, navigation, smartphone projection, emergency calling, remote commands, and OTA services. In most modern platforms the head unit and telematics unit are tightly woven into the vehicle's broader network architecture, not bolted on as an afterthought, which is why an infotainment freeze can sometimes ripple into camera display or even connectivity-diagnostics symptoms.

HU — Head Unit

central

The central infotainment computer running the display, audio control, navigation UI, and most user-facing functions, usually communicating with the rest of the vehicle over CAN or Ethernet.

It typically runs Android Automotive, Linux, QNX, or a proprietary OEM stack, and it's also where RVC/surround-view video and phone integration actually get displayed to the driver.

In practical terms, the HU is the driver's single gateway into the vehicle — most of what "feels like the car's software" is really the HU's UI layer.

TCU — Telematics Control Unit

cellular

Provides cellular connectivity and remote services: eCall/emergency response, remote lock/unlock, vehicle tracking, diagnostics upload, and OTA-related backend communication.

It usually bundles GNSS and modem management too, and it talks to the gateway or a central ECU to fetch or push vehicle data as needed.

Because it's the vehicle's bridge to the outside internet, the TCU is one of the most security-sensitive modules in the whole architecture.

BT / Wi-Fi Module

A2DP·AVRCP·HFP

Handles Bluetooth and Wi-Fi to external devices. Bluetooth covers A2DP (audio streaming), AVRCP (media control), HFP (hands-free calling), and PBAP (phonebook access); Wi-Fi typically carries hotspot, CarPlay, Android Auto, diagnostics, or software-update traffic.

In most platforms this isn't a standalone box — it's baked directly into the infotainment SoC rather than existing as a separate physical module.

Given your Bluetooth validation background, this is the module where profile-level regression testing (HFP call handoff, A2DP dropout under Wi-Fi coexistence) tends to matter most.

DAB / Radio Tuner

RF

The broadcast reception block for digital and conventional radio — it receives RF, demodulates it, and passes audio or metadata to the infotainment system.

It's a separate signal chain from the speakers themselves but closely tied to the HU's UI, and in some architectures the tuner is an external module reached over a digital audio link or Ethernet rather than being built into the HU.

Amplifier / DSP

audio

The amplifier drives the speakers; the DSP handles equalization, delay alignment, surround effects, loudness compensation, and often active noise cancellation support.

In premium systems the amplifier is a smart, addressable node on the vehicle network rather than a dumb analog box — it can accept compressed digital streams and apply cabin-specific tuning on the fly.

This block quietly does more for perceived cabin "quality" than the speakers themselves usually get credit for.

CID / Cluster / HUD

displays

CID is the center info display, Cluster is the instrument cluster, and HUD projects critical information straight into the driver's line of sight — three displays, often three different content sources.

These need synchronized graphics and fast, safety-aware updates, and important ADAS or camera alerts frequently need to appear on more than one display at once so the driver can't miss them.

RSE — Rear Seat Entertainment

rear

Entertainment screens for rear passengers — media playback, phone mirroring, streaming apps, and sometimes independent audio zones.

Depending on the architecture it may hang off the main infotainment system or run semi-independently, and its network traffic is mostly media/control rather than anything real-time-safety-critical.

04

Body & Chassis ECUs

The operational backbone of the car: powertrain, braking, access, and safety, all constantly talking over CAN, CAN-FD, LIN, and increasingly Ethernet, usually mediated by a gateway or zonal controller so no single ECU has to know every other ECU by name.

BCM — Body Control Module

comfort

Manages lighting, wipers, central locking, power windows, mirrors, interior lights, and most other comfort features — one of the most universally present ECUs in any vehicle.

It often acts as the local coordinator for many low-speed devices and also handles diagnostics and wake/sleep behavior for the body electronics domain.

In newer zonal architectures many "BCM functions" migrate to zone controllers, but the logical role — coordinating body-level devices — survives the rename.

ECM / PCM

powertrain

Controls engine and powertrain operation: fuel injection, ignition, throttle, torque requests, emissions, and often hybrid coordination — the module responsible for actually producing motion efficiently and safely.

It takes driver inputs and higher-level system commands and reports back status, torque, and fault data continuously.

In EV/hybrid platforms the naming shifts (sometimes to a "vehicle control unit"), but the core responsibility — turning a torque request into motion — stays the same.

TCM — Transmission Control

gearing

Manages gear selection, shift timing, clutch control, and overall transmission behavior, coordinating constantly with the engine/powertrain controller for smooth, efficient acceleration.

In automatic and dual-clutch systems it's heavily software- and calibration-dependent, exchanging frequent status frames over CAN/CAN-FD — and in some vehicles it's folded into a broader powertrain controller entirely.

ABS / ESC Module

stability

Controls braking stability, wheel-slip management, and electronic stability behavior using wheel-speed sensors to modulate individual brake pressure during hard braking or cornering.

It's also a critical data source for ADAS: lane-keeping and stability-aware driver-assist functions lean on ABS/ESC vehicle-dynamics data to know exactly what the car is currently doing.

SRS — Airbag Control

safety

Monitors crash sensors, seatbelt signals, and occupant status to decide whether and how airbags should deploy — a genuinely safety-critical ECU with strict timing and fault-handling requirements.

It often interfaces directly with OMS or seat-occupancy logic so deployment strategy can adapt to whether a seat holds an adult, a child, or nothing at all.

Because incorrect deployment is a serious risk either way (firing or not firing), this module is one of the most heavily regulated pieces of software in the entire car.

PEPS

keyless

Passive Entry Passive Start — enables keyless entry and keyless engine start by detecting the key fob through short-range communication with vehicle-side antennas.

It typically coordinates with the BCM, gateway/security logic, and immobilizer functions to unlock or start the car without the driver ever touching a physical key.

It's a convenience feature on the surface, but underneath it carries real cybersecurity weight — relay-attack resistance is a standard PEPS validation concern.

Gateway ECU

router

The traffic hub between networks — bridging CAN, LIN, Ethernet, and sometimes legacy buses, filtering messages and enforcing routing and security rules.

It's what prevents every single ECU from needing to know every other ECU directly, and its importance only grows as vehicles move toward zonal architectures with mixed-speed networks.

05

Vehicle Networking Backbone

The communication fabric connecting everything above. The choice of bus is a tradeoff between latency, bandwidth, cost, topology, reliability, diagnostics, and security — and as sensor data volume keeps growing, Ethernet is increasingly the one carrying camera and domain-controller traffic while CAN and LIN quietly hold down control and legacy duties.

CAN / CAN-FD

control

The classic automotive control network: robust, deterministic, simple. Excellent for low-to-medium bandwidth messages like door status, engine requests, switch signals, and most diagnostic interactions.

CAN-FD extends payload size and bitrate for heavier control data, but even in the newest cars, plain CAN remains everywhere simply because it's cheap and thoroughly proven.

LIN

low-cost

A low-cost, low-speed network for simple devices — mirror motors, seats, rain sensors, small body actuators — anywhere the traffic is predictable and bandwidth needs stay low.

LIN typically hangs off a more capable node like a BCM or zone controller; its whole reason for existing is cost, not speed.

Automotive Ethernet

high-BW

Now the backbone for high-bandwidth applications — cameras, ADAS sensors, infotainment, and zonal networking — carrying much higher data rates than CAN, including video, radar data, and synchronized sensor traffic.

For camera systems especially, latency, synchronization, switching behavior, and security become the real design challenges, which is exactly why network architecture choices matter so much for vision quality.

FlexRay

legacy

A legacy deterministic network used for time-critical control before Ethernet matured, offering more determinism than CAN — attractive for certain chassis and powertrain use cases in its day.

It's much less common in new designs now, but still turns up in older premium or safety-oriented architectures.

MOST

legacy

A legacy network built mainly for multimedia transport in older infotainment systems — audio/video distribution and device coordination within the cabin.

Ethernet has largely replaced it in modern vehicles because it scales far better and fits more naturally alongside the rest of the vehicle network.

How These Blocks Work Together

A modern vehicle is a distributed system, not a single computer — nothing here works in isolation. Sensors perceive, ECUs decide, actuators act, and the network connects all of it into something that behaves like one coordinated machine even though it's really dozens of small ones cooperating.

07

What Matters in Validation

From a V&V perspective, the highest-value things to check are almost always field of view, latency, startup time, synchronization, fault behavior, network load, and diagnostics — not just "does the feature work."

Cameras

vision

Image delay, stitching quality (for MVC/SVC), brightness adaptation, and calibration drift over time and temperature.

Radar & Ultrasonic

ranging

Detection range accuracy, object classification confidence, and false-positive / false-negative behavior across weather and lighting conditions.

ECUs & Networking

bus

Message timing, wake/sleep transitions, gateway filtering correctness, DTC behavior, and fail-safe reactions under fault injection.

Study tip: trace one real scenario end to end — like the reverse-parking flow above — instead of memorizing module names in isolation. Following the signal from gear selection through the camera, the display, and back through the gateway makes the whole architecture click into place far faster than a flat glossary ever will.
