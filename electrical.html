Category 1 — Fundamental DC Quantities
The bedrock building blocks: charge, voltage, current, resistance, and power.
Electric Charge
Q
Coulombs (C) Q = I × t Q = C × V

The fundamental property of matter that causes it to experience a force in an electromagnetic field. It is quantised — the smallest unit is the charge of one electron (≈1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C). Charge is the "stuff" that actually flows when we talk about electricity.

Where used: EV battery sizing — a 75 kWh Tesla pack stores charge equivalent to tens of millions of Coulombs, used to calculate how long it can drive at a given current draw.
Voltage (EMF)
V / E
Volts (V) V = I × R V = W / Q V = P / I

The electric potential difference between two points — the "pressure" that pushes charge carriers through a conductor. Think of it like water pressure in a pipe: higher voltage means more push. EMF (Electromotive Force) is the voltage produced by a source like a battery or generator.

Where used: Power grid transmission lines operate at 400 kV to reduce current (and thus I²R losses) over long distances, exploiting the V = IR relationship.
Current
I
Amperes (A) I = V / R I = Q / t I = P / V

The rate of flow of electric charge past a point in a circuit. One Ampere equals one Coulomb of charge flowing per second. Conventional current flows from positive to negative; actual electrons flow the opposite way (from negative to positive).

Where used: PCB trace width design — engineers calculate maximum current per trace to avoid copper melting, using PCB trace current capacity charts based on I = Q/t.
Resistance
R
Ohms (Ω) R = V / I R = ρL / A R = 1 / G

The opposition to the flow of direct current, converting electrical energy into heat. It depends on the material's resistivity (ρ), its length (L), and cross-sectional area (A). Unlike reactance, resistance dissipates real energy regardless of frequency.

Where used: LED current-limiting resistors — a 220 Ω resistor in series with an LED on a 5V supply limits current to ~(5-2.2)/220 ≈ 12.7 mA, protecting the LED.
Conductance
G
Siemens (S) G = 1 / R G = I / V

The ease with which DC current flows — the exact reciprocal of resistance. A high conductance means very little opposition. A 2 Ω resistor has G = 0.5 S. Conductance belongs to the "admittance family" (Y = G + jB) in AC circuits, representing only the real, energy-dissipating part.

Where used: Ground-fault circuit analysis — parallel branch conductances are added directly (Gtotal = G₁ + G₂ + …), making fault path calculations cleaner than summing resistances in parallel.
Power (DC)
P
Watts (W) P = V × I P = I² × R P = V² / R

The rate at which electrical energy is converted into another form (heat, light, mechanical work). In DC circuits, all power is "real" power — it actually does work. The three forms of the formula are all equivalent via Ohm's Law.

Where used: Calculating I²R heat losses in a power supply MOSFET — a 10 mΩ FET carrying 20 A dissipates P = 20² × 0.01 = 4 W, which must be managed with a heatsink.
Energy
W
Joules (J) / Wh W = P × t W = V × Q W = ½CV²

The total work done or the amount of power consumed over a period of time. Energy is the capacity to do work. In electrical billing, kilowatt-hours (kWh) are used: 1 kWh = 3.6 MJ. A 100 W bulb on for 10 hours uses 1 kWh of energy.

Where used: Electricity billing meters (smart meters) — they integrate power over time (W = P × t) to measure kWh consumed by a household for billing purposes.
Category 2 — AC & Reactive Quantities: The "Opposition" Family
Frequency, phase, reactance, and impedance — how AC circuits fight back.
Frequency & Period
f / T
Hz / Seconds (s) f = 1 / T ω = 2πf

Frequency (f) is the number of complete AC cycles per second. Period (T) is the time for one full cycle — they are exact reciprocals. Angular frequency ω (rad/s) = 2πf is used in reactance formulas. Mains frequency is 50 Hz (UK/India) or 60 Hz (USA).

Where used: Power grid synchronisation — all generators on a grid must run at exactly 50.00 Hz. A 0.5 Hz deviation triggers automatic load shedding to prevent grid collapse.
Phase Angle
θ (phi)
Degrees (°) / Radians θ = arctan(X/R) PF = cos θ

The time difference between the voltage and current waveforms in an AC circuit, expressed as an angle. A purely resistive circuit has θ = 0° (V and I perfectly in phase). An ideal inductor has θ = +90° (current lags voltage). An ideal capacitor has θ = −90° (current leads voltage).

Where used: Industrial motor drives — a lagging phase angle (inductive motor load) means poor power factor; capacitor banks are switched in to reduce θ and improve PF, lowering electricity bills.
Inductance
L
Henries (H) V = L × (dI/dt) W = ½LI²

A coil's ability to oppose changes in current by storing energy in a magnetic field. When current changes, the magnetic field induces a back-EMF that fights that change (Lenz's Law). A 1 H inductor generates 1 V when current changes at 1 A/s. Real inductors also have series resistance (DCR).

Where used: Switching power supply (SMPS) output filter — a 22 µH inductor smooths the pulsed output of a buck converter into a steady DC voltage for a USB charger.
Capacitance
C
Farads (F) C = Q / V W = ½CV²

A capacitor's ability to store electric charge by creating an electric field between two conductors. A 1 F capacitor stores 1 Coulomb of charge when 1 Volt is applied. Capacitors block DC but pass AC — the higher the frequency, the more current they allow through (lower reactance).

Where used: DRAM memory cells — each bit is stored as charge on a tiny ~30 fF capacitor. Billions of these on a single chip form your computer's RAM.
Inductive Reactance
XL
Ohms (Ω) XL = 2πfL = ωL

The frequency-dependent opposition to AC current offered by an inductor. Unlike resistance, it stores and returns energy rather than dissipating it as heat. Crucially, XL increases with frequency: at high frequencies, an inductor becomes nearly an open circuit. At DC (f = 0), XL = 0 — a wire.

Where used: EMI filter chokes on mains cables — a high XL at RF frequencies (MHz range) blocks high-frequency noise from entering appliances, while passing 50 Hz mains current freely.
Capacitive Reactance
XC
Ohms (Ω) XC = 1 / (2πfC)

The frequency-dependent opposition to AC current offered by a capacitor. It decreases with increasing frequency: at high frequencies, a capacitor is nearly a short circuit. At DC (f = 0), XC → ∞ — an open circuit. This is the opposite behaviour to XL, making LC circuits resonate.

Where used: Audio crossover networks — a series capacitor (high XC at low f) blocks bass from tweeters, while a series inductor (high XL at high f) blocks treble from woofers.
Impedance
Z
Ohms (Ω) Z = R + j(XL−XC) |Z| = √(R²+X²)

The total opposition to AC current — the AC generalisation of resistance. Impedance is a complex number combining the real part (Resistance R, which dissipates energy) and the imaginary part (Net Reactance X = XL − XC, which stores energy). Think of it as the hypotenuse of the impedance triangle.

Where used: Antenna design — a 50 Ω coaxial cable must be impedance-matched to an antenna. Mismatched impedance causes signal reflections, measured by the Standing Wave Ratio (SWR).
Category 3 — The "Reciprocal" AC Quantities
Admittance, Conductance (AC), and Susceptance — the flip-side of the opposition family.
Reciprocal of Impedance
Admittance
Y
Siemens (S) Y = 1 / Z Y = G + jB |Y| = 1 / |Z|

The total ease with which AC current flows through a circuit — the exact reciprocal of Impedance (Z). Just as impedance Z = R + jX (opposition), admittance Y = G + jB (ease of flow). G is the conductance (ease despite resistance) and B is the susceptance (ease despite reactance). Admittance makes parallel circuit analysis much simpler — parallel admittances add directly, just like parallel conductances.

Where used: RF circuit design and power systems — admittance (Y-parameters) simplify analysis of parallel-connected loads on a bus, making power flow calculations tractable in large grids.
Reciprocal of Resistance
Conductance (AC context)
G
Siemens (S) G = 1 / R G = Re(Y)

In the admittance framework (Y = G + jB), G is the real part of admittance — it represents pure, DC-like ease of current flow due to resistance alone. It dissipates real energy as heat. Crucially, G is NOT the reciprocal of |Z| in a complex circuit; it specifically reciprocates only the resistive (real) component R. A 4 Ω resistor has G = 0.25 S.

Where used: Transmission line modelling — the shunt conductance G (S/m) models leakage current through imperfect insulation along coaxial cables and power lines.
Reciprocal of Reactance
Susceptance
B
Siemens (S) B = 1 / X B = Im(Y)

The imaginary part of admittance — the ease with which AC current flows through the reactive part of a circuit. It is the reciprocal of reactance (B = 1/X). Susceptance does NOT dissipate energy; it only stores and returns it. The sign convention is the opposite of reactance: capacitive susceptance is positive, inductive susceptance is negative.

BC — Capacitive Susceptance (Positive)
BC = 2πfC = ωC
BC is positive and increases with frequency and capacitance. A larger capacitor or higher frequency means more current can flow through the reactive path. BC = 1/XC, and since XC decreases with f, BC increases — the capacitor becomes more "transparent" to current.
BL — Inductive Susceptance (Negative)
BL = −1 / (2πfL) = −1/(ωL)
BL is negative and its magnitude decreases as frequency or inductance increases. A large inductor at high frequency has a tiny BL — it strongly opposes current flow. The negative sign correctly places it in the 4th quadrant of the admittance phasor diagram, opposing BC.
Where used: Static VAR Compensators (SVC) on power grids — thyristor-controlled reactors tune BL and BC in real-time to maintain grid voltage stability, compensating for reactive power swings.
Category 4 — AC Power & Efficiency
Real, reactive, and apparent power — the power triangle explained.
Real Power (Active Power)
P
Watts (W) P = V×I×cos θ P = S×PF P = I²R

The actual power that performs useful work — converted irreversibly into heat, light, or mechanical energy. The cos θ factor (power factor) reduces it from the apparent power. Only the resistive component of a circuit consumes real power. Real power is what your electricity meter bills you for.

Where used: Data centre power planning — IT equipment is rated in Watts (real power), but UPS systems must supply VA. A server drawing 500 W at PF 0.9 requires 556 VA from its UPS.
Reactive Power
Q
VAr (Volt-Ampere reactive) Q = V×I×sin θ Q = √(S²−P²)

The power that oscillates back and forth between source and reactive components (inductors, capacitors) without doing useful work. It's "borrowed" from the source and returned each half-cycle. Inductive loads (motors) generate positive Q (lagging); capacitive loads generate negative Q (leading). Q represents the tax on the supply system — it heats wires without doing work.

Where used: Industrial power factor correction — a factory with inductive motors generating +50 kVAr adds capacitor banks generating −50 kVAr to cancel reactive power, reducing electricity charges.
Apparent Power
S
VA (Volt-Amperes) S = V × I S = √(P²+Q²) S = P + jQ

The vector sum of real power (P) and reactive power (Q) — what the supply source "feels" it is delivering. It is the hypotenuse of the power triangle: S² = P² + Q². Apparent power determines the size of generators, transformers, and cables, even though only P does useful work. Rated in VA, not Watts, to emphasise this distinction.

Where used: Transformer nameplate ratings — a 100 kVA distribution transformer can supply 100 kVA of apparent power, but how much of that is useful (kW) depends on the load's power factor.
Power Factor
PF
Dimensionless (0 to 1) PF = P / S PF = cos θ

The ratio of real power (P) to apparent power (S) — a measure of how efficiently a circuit converts supplied power into useful work. PF = 1.0 (unity) is ideal: all supplied power does useful work, zero reactive current. PF = 0 (purely reactive load) means no real work is done. Poor PF forces larger conductors and equipment to carry the extra reactive current.

Where used: Electricity tariffs for large commercial users — industries with PF below 0.95 are penalised by utilities, incentivising capacitor bank installation to correct the lagging PF of motor loads.
